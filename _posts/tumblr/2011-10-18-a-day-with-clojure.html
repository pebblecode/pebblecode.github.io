It started with trying to find something to do for 10% time - there were some technologies I wanted to play with - but in a twist of fate Heroku announced it’s support of Clojure on their cloud service. That really got my attention.

Clojure is a functional programming language, with Lisp like syntax, built on the Java JVM. I’ve always wanted to get back into some form of functional programming, ever since experiencing the elegance of Haskell in 1st year uni. I’ve also been hearing advice here and there to learn different paradigms of programming, as a way of improving your programming ability - which I really agree with. I was already learning ruby and objective c, so I had dynamically typed and statically typed languages covered. Functional was the main other paradigm I was missing.

The problem I find with functional languages like Haskell though, is that they seem to be stuck within the domain of academia, or very obscure problem domains. I prefer pragmatic languages that have more usage in industry. Heroku’s support was a great plus on that side of things.

My day with Clojure mainly revolved around getting started with Clojure, getting it deployed, checking out the community of Clojure, and seeing what it can be used for.

In terms of getting started and deployed, Heroku made it very easy with their Getting started guide. I think I was all done within 20mins. The dependency framework, leiningen, much like bundler for ruby, saved me a lot of time in setting up the environment.

I was quite surprised by the number of web frameworks already available and ready to be used. However, after some cursory coding, it did feel a little clunky to work in a functional paradigm for the web. Ruby seems like a more agile and intuitive fit, although this may be my familiarity bias. 

Apart from the web side of things, I also encountered clojure koans which were inspired by ruby koans (also great if you don’t already know of them), that really helped to get a grasp of the concepts of clojure. The screencasts by Rich Hickey (the creator of clojure) on itunes are also apparently pretty good at teaching the concepts (although, I haven’t had time to go through many of them). Full disclojure is also another screencast resource I kept hearing about.

Some other tidbits I found along the way were a survey on industry usage, an in-browser tool to try out clojure, and a news site on the latest in clojure.

Although, I didn’t end up doing much juicy development in clojure by the end of the day, I did get a good taste of what clojure was about. The community seemed quite friendly, and there’s an exciting vibe coming from them. I’m keen to look into it further - maybe not necessarily for web development, but I’m sure there are some other good use cases around. I definitely agree with heroku’s approach of supporting the right tool for the job, and Clojure seems like a great tool to add in my developer toolbox.

Update: I went to the LCUG incanter event, and was very humbled by the very smart and interesting people there - I’ll definitely try and attend more of these. There are frequent clojure dojos happening in london, for those interested too.
